## Why this document exists

AlphaDesk presents many numbers: analyst confidence, growth scores, valuation gaps, consensus confidence, STARS, volatility, position limits, and debate conviction. Those numbers do not all mean the same thing.

This document answers five questions for every part of the system:

1. What question is it trying to answer?
2. What data can it see?
3. What formula or judgment produces the result?
4. What does the displayed number actually mean?
5. What limitations or implementation details should we remember when interpreting it?

The most important principle is:

> A confidence percentage is a model score, not a calibrated probability that the stock will go up or that the analyst is correct.

---

## Executive summary

AlphaDesk has two separate research systems.

### Signals

Signals is the formula-driven council shown on the signal card. It has:

- 11 registered analysts;
- 9 analysts enabled in the normal default run;
- 8 deterministic default analysts;
- 1 LLM-assisted default analyst, Warren Buffett;
- 2 optional analysts, Social Sentiment and Adaptive Research;
- a deterministic consensus calculation;
- a deterministic risk manager; and
- a deterministic portfolio manager.

The default nine are Technical, Fundamentals, SEC Filings, Web Research, Growth, Valuation, News Sentiment, Insider Sentiment, and Warren Buffett.

### Debate

Debate is a question-driven research pod. A supervisor assigns work to four specialists, a bear challenges them, and a synthesizer produces a memo. Its stance, finding-confidence labels, memo conviction, and directional lean are LLM judgments constrained by schemas and evidence rules. They are not generated by fixed arithmetic formulas.

### The four numbers most likely to be misunderstood

| Number | What it really means | What it does **not** mean |
|---|---|---|
| Analyst confidence | Strength produced by that analyst's own scoring rule | Probability the analyst is correct |
| Consensus weighted score | Average signed analyst confidence, with neutral analysts contributing zero | A weighted average of target prices or expected return |
| Consensus confidence | Average confidence reported by all participating analysts, including neutral analysts | Confidence in the consensus direction |
| STARS | A label derived only from the consensus weighted score | Independent investment research or a separate model |

## 1. End-to-end flow

For each run, AlphaDesk does this:

1. Fetch one market-data snapshot.
2. Give each analyst only the slice of data its contract permits.
3. Run each selected analyst once per ticker.
4. Record each result as `bullish`, `bearish`, `neutral`, `abstained`, or `failed`.
5. Combine usable analyst results into consensus.
6. Convert consensus into a 1–5 STARS rating.
7. Calculate volatility- and correlation-based position limits.
8. Convert consensus plus limits into `buy`, `sell`, or `hold`.
9. Optionally ask an LLM to explain already-final deterministic results. This narration cannot change the numbers.

Every saved Signals run contains the snapshot, exact views, signals, LLM calls, and final recommendation. This makes a historical result reproducible from its saved inputs even when live data later changes.

## 2. Data sources and freshness

| Evidence | Primary source | Fallback / processing | Used by |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily OHLCV prices | Financial Datasets | Yahoo Finance; cached | Technical, Risk |
| Financial ratios and growth metrics | Financial Datasets, requested as TTM, up to 10 periods | Yahoo annual statements, usually fewer periods | Fundamentals, Growth, Valuation, Buffett |
| Financial line items | Financial Datasets | Yahoo annual statements | Valuation, Buffett |
| Market cap | Financial Datasets company facts or latest metric | Yahoo Finance | Valuation, Buffett |
| Company news | Financial Datasets, up to 25 items in the snapshot | Yahoo Finance | News Sentiment, Buffett, Adaptive Research |
| Insider trades | Financial Datasets, up to 100 items in the snapshot | Yahoo Finance | Insider Sentiment, Buffett |
| SEC passages | Latest 10-K/10-Q from SEC EDGAR | HTML cleanup, chunking, BM25-style retrieval, local cache | SEC Filings, Adaptive Research, Debate |
| Web results | Tavily | DuckDuckGo | Web Research, Adaptive Research |
| Reddit | Reddit API | fresh local cache, Bright Data, then stale local cache | Social Sentiment |
| Community posts | Moomoo community feed | quality and language filtering | Social Sentiment |

Important data-order rule: financial metrics and line items are treated as **newest first**. `metrics[0]` and `line_items[0]` mean the latest available period.

Primary and fallback data are not perfectly equivalent. For example, the primary financial request is TTM, while Yahoo's fallback builds multi-period metrics from annual statements and overlays live valuation ratios only on the newest row. A result can therefore change because the source changed even when the company did not.

## 3. Shared result vocabulary

### Direction

- **Bullish:** evidence crossed that analyst's positive threshold.
- **Bearish:** evidence crossed that analyst's negative threshold.
- **Neutral:** the analyst completed but its score stayed between thresholds.
- **Abstained:** required data was unavailable or insufficient. It does not vote.
- **Failed:** execution or parsing failed. It does not vote.

### Confidence

Each analyst has its own confidence formula. Therefore, `95%` from Valuation and `95%` from Fundamentals are not directly comparable measurements. They merely share the same 0–100 display scale.

### Explain reasoning

For deterministic analysts, the optional LLM reasoning switch receives the final direction, final confidence, and key factors and writes a short explanation. It cannot modify direction, confidence, status, or factors. Warren Buffett and Adaptive Research are exceptions because an LLM participates in producing their original signal.

# Part I — Signals analysts

## 4. Technical Analyst

### Question

What is the recent price action saying about trend, momentum, mean reversion, and risk regime?

### Inputs

Daily open, high, low, close, and volume for the selected date window.

### Five sub-strategies

| Strategy | Council weight | Main calculation | Bullish | Bearish | Otherwise |
|---|---:|---|---|---|---|
| Trend | 25% | EMA 8, 21, 55 and 14-day ADX | EMA8 > EMA21 > EMA55 | EMA8 < EMA21 < EMA55 | Neutral |
| Momentum | 25% | `0.4 × 21-day return sum + 0.3 × 63-day sum + 0.3 × 126-day sum` | score > 5% and current volume > 21-day average | score < -5% and volume confirms | Neutral |
| Mean reversion | 20% | 50-day price Z-score and position inside Bollinger Bands | Z < -2 and band position < 0.2 | Z > 2 and band position > 0.8 | Neutral |
| Volatility | 15% | 21-day annualized volatility relative to its 63-day history | regime < 0.8 and volatility Z < -1 | regime > 1.2 and volatility Z > 1 | Neutral |
| Statistical arbitrage | 15% | Hurst exponent and 63-day return skew | Hurst < 0.4 and skew > 1 | Hurst < 0.4 and skew < -1 | Neutral |

Sub-strategy confidence is:

- Trend: `ADX / 100`.
- Momentum: `min(abs(momentum score) × 5, 1)`.
- Mean reversion: `min(abs(price Z-score) / 4, 1)`.
- Volatility: `min(abs(volatility Z-score) / 3, 1)`.
- Statistical arbitrage: `(0.5 - Hurst) × 2` when directional.
- Every neutral sub-strategy receives `50%` confidence.

### Final formula

Convert bullish to `+1`, neutral to `0`, and bearish to `-1`.

```text
technical score =
    Σ(direction value × strategy weight × sub-strategy confidence)
    ÷ Σ(strategy weight × sub-strategy confidence)
```

- Bullish when technical score > `+0.20`.
- Bearish when technical score < `-0.20`.
- Neutral otherwise.
- Final confidence = `absolute value of technical score`.

### How to read it

`technical bullish 42%` means the signed ensemble score was approximately `+0.42`. It does not mean a 42% probability of an increase.

### Audit notes

- Neutral strategies still add 50% confidence to the denominator, which dilutes directional strategies.
- RSI-14 and RSI-28 are calculated for Mean Reversion but do not affect its decision.
- Momentum uses sums of daily percentage returns, not compounded period returns.
- There is no explicit minimum-history check. Rolling indicators can become `NaN` on short histories and usually fall through to neutral behavior.
- The statistical-arbitrage component does not compare the stock with another security; it is a single-series Hurst/skew rule.

## 5. Fundamentals Analyst

### Question

Does the latest reported period show a profitable, growing, financially healthy, reasonably valued company?

### Inputs

The newest financial-metrics row. Historical rows and raw line items are available in the view but are not used by this analyst.

### Four categories

Each category has three yes/no checks and therefore scores from 0 to 3.

| Category | One point for each condition |
|---|---|
| Profitability | ROE > 15%; net margin > 20%; operating margin > 15% |
| Growth | revenue growth > 10%; earnings growth > 10%; book-value growth > 10% |
| Health | current ratio > 1.5; debt/equity < 0.5; FCF/share > 80% of EPS |
| Basic valuation | P/E < 25; P/B < 3; P/S < 5 |

A category is called bullish when it scores at least `2/3`. A category is called bearish only when it scores exactly `0/3`. A `1/3` category is neither.

### Final rule

- Bullish if at least 3 of 4 categories are bullish.
- Bearish if at least 3 of 4 categories are bearish.
- Neutral otherwise.

For a directional result:

```text
confidence = min(95%, 50% + 15% × confirming categories)
```

Therefore 3 confirming categories produce 95%, and 4 also remain capped at 95%. Neutral is always 50%.

### How to read it

`fundamentals bullish 95%` normally means at least three categories scored at least two checks each. It is a threshold score, not a statistical confidence interval.

### Audit notes

- Missing fields fail their yes/no checks. Once at least one metrics row exists, missing data can push categories toward zero instead of causing an abstention.
- The same absolute ratio thresholds are used for every industry. Banks, retailers, software firms, and industrial companies are not normalized by sector.
- Valuation ratios are included here even though a separate Valuation Analyst exists, so valuation influences the council through more than one analyst.
- The confidence scale jumps directly from neutral 50% to directional 95%; there is little gradation.

## 6. Growth Analyst

### Question

Is growth strong, improving, reasonably priced, margin-supported, and financially sustainable?

### Inputs

At least four financial-metrics periods, newest first. If fewer than four exist, the analyst abstains.

### Component weights

```text
growth score =
    45% × growth-trend score
  + 25% × growth-valuation score
  + 20% × margin score
  + 10% × financial-health score
```

Each component is intended to sit between 0 and 1.

### Growth-trend score, maximum 1.00

- Latest revenue growth > 20%: `+0.40`; else if > 10%: `+0.20`.
- Positive revenue-growth trend slope: `+0.10`.
- Latest EPS growth > 20%: `+0.25`; else if > 10%: `+0.10`.
- Positive EPS-growth trend slope: `+0.05`.
- Latest FCF growth > 15%: `+0.10`.

The trend slope is an ordinary least-squares slope over the supplied sequence.

### Growth-valuation score, maximum 1.00

- PEG < 1: `+0.50`; else if PEG < 2: `+0.25`.
- P/S < 2: `+0.50`; else if P/S < 5: `+0.25`.

### Margin score, maximum 1.00

- Latest gross margin > 50%: `+0.20`.
- Positive gross-margin trend slope: `+0.20`.
- Latest operating margin > 15%: `+0.20`.
- Positive operating-margin trend slope: `+0.20`.
- Positive net-margin trend slope: `+0.20`.

### Financial-health score

Starts at `1.00`.

- Debt/equity > 1.5: subtract `0.50`.
- Else debt/equity > 0.8: subtract `0.20`.
- Current ratio < 1.0: subtract `0.50`.
- Else current ratio < 1.5: subtract `0.20`.
- Floor at zero.

### Final rule

- Bullish when weighted score > `0.60`.
- Bearish when weighted score < `0.40`.
- Neutral from `0.40` through `0.60`, inclusive.
- Confidence = `min(95%, abs(weighted score - 0.50) × 2)`.

Examples:

- Score `0.80` → bullish, 60% confidence.
- Score `0.65` → bullish, 30% confidence.
- Score `0.50` → neutral, 0% confidence.
- Score `0.20` → bearish, 60% confidence.

### Audit notes

- **Trend-direction issue:** the data is newest first, but the slope is calculated from index 0 toward older periods. A positive slope therefore often means older values were higher, even though the code labels it “accelerating.” This can reverse the intended trend interpretation.
- Missing PEG, P/S, or margin fields contribute zero rather than reducing the available maximum. Partial data can therefore look bearish.
- Financial health begins at a perfect score and remains perfect when leverage or liquidity fields are missing.
- The analyst is called “Growth,” but 55% of its final weight comes from valuation, margins, and health.

## 7. Valuation Analyst

### Question

How does a blend of four model-implied equity values compare with the company's current market capitalization?

### Inputs

Financial metrics, at least two line-item periods, and market cap. The model abstains if these minimums are unavailable.

### Important interpretation

The analyst calculates company-level values and compares them with market cap. The displayed method values are not 12-month market-price forecasts. The 12-month target has been removed from the product.

### Model weights

| Model | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Enhanced DCF | 35% |
| Owner Earnings | 35% |
| EV/EBITDA | 20% |
| Residual Income | 10% |

If a model returns zero or a negative value, it is removed and the remaining weights are renormalized.

### 7.1 Cost of equity and WACC

The default CAPM cost of equity is:

```text
cost of equity = risk-free rate + beta proxy × market risk premium
               = 4.5% + 1.0 × 6.0%
               = 10.5%
```

Net debt is:

```text
net debt = max(total debt - cash, 0)
```

Estimated cost of debt is:

```text
if interest coverage is available:
    cost of debt = max(5.5%, 4.5% + 10 / interest coverage)
otherwise:
    cost of debt = 9.5%
```

Then:

```text
WACC = equity weight × cost of equity
     + debt weight × cost of debt × (1 - 25% tax rate)
```

The final WACC is clipped to a 6% floor and a 20% cap.

### 7.2 Enhanced DCF

The DCF uses free cash flow history.

```text
FCF volatility = standard deviation of positive FCF / mean positive FCF
quality factor = max(0.70, 1 - 0.50 × FCF volatility)
base FCF = max(latest FCF, 85% × average of latest three FCF periods)
```

Growth assumptions:

- Base growth starts from latest revenue growth, defaulting to 5%.
- It is capped at 25%.
- For companies above $50 billion market cap, it is capped at 10%.
- Years 1–3 use the high-growth rate.
- Years 4–7 use declining transition rates.
- Terminal growth is the lower of 3% and 60% of high growth.

For each forecast year:

```text
present value of FCF in year t = projected FCF_t / (1 + WACC)^t
```

Terminal value uses the Gordon-growth formula:

```text
terminal value = final FCF × (1 + terminal growth) / (WACC - terminal growth)
```

The result is multiplied by the quality factor.

Three scenarios are calculated:

| Scenario | Revenue-growth multiplier | WACC multiplier | Blend weight |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Bear | 0.5× | 1.2× | 20% |
| Base | 1.0× | 1.0× | 60% |
| Bull | 1.5× | 0.9× | 20% |

```text
DCF expected value = 20% × bear + 60% × base + 20% × bull
```

### 7.3 Owner Earnings

```text
owner earnings = net income + depreciation - capex - change in working capital
```

Owner earnings are grown for five years using latest earnings growth, or 5% if missing, and discounted at a fixed required return of 15%.

Terminal growth is capped at 3%. After discounting the forecast and terminal value, the result receives a 25% margin-of-safety haircut:

```text
owner-earnings value = discounted intrinsic value × 75%
```

### 7.4 EV/EBITDA

Current EBITDA is inferred from current enterprise value and the current EV/EBITDA ratio:

```text
current EBITDA = current enterprise value / current EV/EBITDA
```

The model applies the median EV/EBITDA ratio across available historical metric periods:

```text
implied enterprise value = historical median multiple × current EBITDA
implied equity value = implied enterprise value - current net debt
```

### 7.5 Residual Income

Book value is inferred from market cap and price/book:

```text
book value = market cap / price-to-book
initial residual income = net income - 10% × book value
```

Positive residual income grows for five years using book-value growth, defaulting to 3%, and is discounted at a fixed 10% cost of equity. Terminal growth is 3%. The final intrinsic value receives a 20% margin-of-safety haircut.

### Blended result

For every valid method:

```text
method gap = (method value - market cap) / market cap
```

```text
weighted valuation gap = Σ(method weight × method gap) / Σ(valid weights)
```

This is mathematically equivalent to calculating a weighted blended company value and then comparing it with market cap.

- Bullish when gap > `+15%`.
- Bearish when gap < `-15%`.
- Neutral otherwise.
- Confidence = `min(95%, absolute gap / 30%)`.

Examples:

- Gap `-57.5%` → bearish, capped at 95% confidence.
- Gap `-20%` → bearish, about 67% confidence.
- Gap `+10%` → neutral, about 33% confidence.

### How to read it

If market cap is `$1.60T` and blended model value is `$680B`, the analyst is saying its assumption set values the equity about 57.5% below the market's current price. It is not saying the stock will trade there in 12 months.

### Audit notes

- **Cost-of-debt concern:** `10 / interest coverage` is used as a decimal rate. At 20× coverage this adds 50 percentage points, not 0.5 percentage points. This can push WACC to its 20% cap and materially depress DCF values.
- Debt/equity is passed into the WACC helper but is not used by its formula.
- Working capital, total debt, and cash are not included in the primary default line-item request. Unless the source returns extra fields or the Yahoo fallback supplies them, Owner Earnings assumes zero working-capital change and WACC assumes zero net debt.
- The DCF receives revenue, FCF, and earnings-growth metrics, but only revenue growth is actually used.
- Scenario configuration includes a terminal-growth adjustment, but that adjustment is never applied.
- DCF value is compared directly with equity market cap without an explicit cash/debt bridge. The interpretation depends on whether the supplied FCF is treated as equity FCF.
- The base FCF uses the greater of latest FCF and 85% of the three-period average. That protects against one depressed year but is not always the most conservative base.
- Working-capital change is used by Owner Earnings, but `working_capital` is not in the primary default line-item request. It may therefore default to zero unless the fallback supplies it.
- EV/EBITDA is the company's own historical-median multiple, not a peer-comparable model. It will often remain close to current market value.
- Residual Income derives book value from market cap divided by P/B, so it is partly anchored to a market-derived input.
- Once the absolute gap exceeds 28.5%, confidence is capped at 95%; a -30% gap and a -70% gap display the same confidence.

## 8. News Sentiment Analyst

### Question

Does the recent headline set lean positive or negative?

### Inputs

Up to 25 recent news items. Price history is included in the view but is not currently used.

### Per-headline classification

- If the provider supplies `positive`, `negative`, or `neutral`, use it with 70% weight.
- Otherwise use a fixed headline lexicon.
- Keyword confidence is `min(80%, 55% + 10% × matching terms)`.
- A keyword tie is neutral at 50%.

### Final formula

```text
net news score =
    (sum of positive headline weights - sum of negative headline weights)
    / total headline count
```

- Bullish when net > `+0.10`.
- Bearish when net < `-0.10`.
- Neutral otherwise.
- Confidence = `min(95%, 50% + absolute net)`.

Because `net` is represented from 0 to 1, a net of `+0.30` becomes 80% confidence.

### Audit notes

- Neutral headlines count in the denominator and dilute positive or negative headlines.
- The title is analyzed, not the article body.
- Source credibility, duplicate stories, publication recency, and market reaction are not weighted.
- Neutral always has at least 50% confidence unless the analyst abstains.

## 9. Insider Sentiment Analyst

### Question

Are reported insider transactions mostly buys or mostly sells?

### Inputs

Up to 100 insider transactions from the snapshot.

### Formula

A positive `transaction_shares` value is a buy; a negative value is a sell.

```text
insider net = (number of buys - number of sells) / classified transactions
```

- Bullish when net > `+0.10`.
- Bearish when net < `-0.10`.
- Neutral otherwise.
- Confidence = `min(95%, 50% + absolute net)`.

Example: 8 buys and 2 sells produce `(8 - 2) / 10 = +0.60`, bullish at 95% confidence after the cap.

### Audit notes

- Transactions are count-weighted, not dollar-weighted. A small purchase counts the same as a very large purchase.
- Grants, exercises, tax sales, planned sales, and open-market trades are not distinguished.
- Multiple transactions by one insider each count separately.
- The rule can reach 95% with a very small sample, such as one buy and no sells.

## 10. Web Research Analyst

### Question

Do live search titles and snippets contain more constructive language or risk language?

### Inputs

Web search results from Tavily or DuckDuckGo, including title, snippet, URL, and source.

### Formula

The analyst counts occurrences from two fixed lexicons. Constructive terms include `beat`, `growth`, `raised`, `record`, and `upgrade`. Risk terms include `decline`, `downgrade`, `investigation`, `regulation`, and `slowdown`.

```text
web score = (constructive term count - risk term count)
            / max(total matched terms, 1)
```

- Bullish when score ≥ `+0.15`.
- Bearish when score ≤ `-0.15`.
- Neutral otherwise.

```text
confidence = min(84%, 50% + 38% × abs(score) + 1% × min(source count, 8))
```

### Audit notes

- This is lexicon counting, not semantic fact checking.
- Repeated terms can dominate the result; sources are not de-duplicated or credibility-weighted here.
- More search results increase confidence even when they repeat the same story.
- A word such as “risk” in “risk declined” still counts as a risk term.
- Citations make the evidence inspectable, but citation presence does not prove the snippet supports the direction.

## 11. SEC Filings Analyst

### Question

Do retrieved passages from the latest official filings contain more constructive language or risk language?

### Inputs

Retrieved chunks from the latest 10-K and 10-Q. Filings are cleaned, divided into roughly 1,400-character chunks, labeled by Item section, and ranked against standard business, risk, liquidity, and outlook queries.

### Formula

The analyst counts fixed positive and negative terms in the retrieved passages.

```text
filing score = (positive term count - negative term count)
               / max(total matched terms, 1)
```

- Bullish when score ≥ `+0.15`.
- Bearish when score ≤ `-0.15`.
- Neutral otherwise.

```text
confidence = min(82%, 50% + 45% × abs(score) + 1% × min(excerpt count, 10))
```

### Audit notes

- Risk Factors are intentionally full of negative terminology. Retrieval mix can therefore determine the stance as much as company performance does.
- The model counts words without understanding negation, materiality, or whether a risk is boilerplate.
- More retrieved passages raise confidence regardless of passage quality.
- The citations are high-quality primary-source links, but the direction remains a simple lexical score.

## 12. Social Sentiment Analyst — optional

### Question

What does recent retail-investor discussion imply, after weighting Reddit posts by engagement?

### Inputs

Reddit post titles, bodies, top comments, upvotes, comment counts, and Moomoo community text.

### Formula

Text is scored by VADER with a finance-specific slang overlay. The compound score lies between -1 and +1.

For Reddit:

```text
post weight = 1 + max(upvotes, 0) + comment count
```

Community posts have weight 1. Non-English-dominant posts are skipped.

```text
social score = Σ(text sentiment × engagement weight) / Σ(engagement weight)
```

- Bullish when score ≥ `+0.15`.
- Bearish when score ≤ `-0.15`.
- Neutral otherwise.

```text
confidence = min(
    85%,
    45% + 40% × abs(score) + 1.5% × min(mention count, 20)
)
```

### Audit notes

- One highly engaged thread can dominate the entire score.
- Upvotes are floored at zero, but comment counts always increase weight even when the comments disagree with the post.
- Reddit titles, bodies, and comments are concatenated into one sentiment value, hiding internal disagreement.
- Moomoo posts receive equal weight because engagement metadata is unavailable.
- This analyst is registered but not included in the default run.

## 13. Adaptive Research Analyst — optional, LLM-assisted

### Question

What should be researched for this specific ticker, and what conclusion does the retrieved evidence support?

### Workflow

1. An LLM creates two web queries and two filing queries.
2. AlphaDesk executes up to three web queries and up to four filing queries.
3. Results are de-duplicated and capped at eight web results and eight filing excerpts.
4. An LLM returns `bullish`, `bearish`, or `neutral`, confidence from 0–100, reasoning, and cited findings.

There is no deterministic direction or confidence formula. The number is the synthesis model's structured judgment.

### Fallback

If retrieval succeeds but synthesis fails, the result is neutral at 50%. If no evidence is retrieved, the analyst abstains.

### Audit notes

- Confidence is model-authored and not calibrated against historical accuracy.
- Different providers or model versions can produce different judgments from the same evidence.
- A stable seed improves repeatability for providers that honor it but does not guarantee identical results.
- The analyst can overlap with Web Research and SEC Filings, causing the same underlying evidence to influence consensus multiple times.
- This analyst is registered but not included in the default run.

## 14. Warren Buffett Analyst — default, LLM-assisted

### Question

Does the company combine quality, financial resilience, shareholder friendliness, and a margin of safety?

### Inputs

Financial metrics, line items, market cap, headline count, and insider-trade count.

### Deterministic scoring

| Component | Raw maximum | Weight | Weighted maximum |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| ROE consistency | 2 | 1.0 | 2.0 |
| Margin strength | 3 | 1.0 | 3.0 |
| Debt/equity | 2 | 1.0 | 2.0 |
| Current ratio | 1 | 0.5 | 0.5 |
| Gross-margin stability | 2 | 1.0 | 2.0 |
| Capital intensity | 2 | 0.8 | 1.6 |
| Book-value growth | 2 | 1.0 | 2.0 |
| Share dilution | 2 | 0.8 | 1.6 |
| FCF conversion | 2 | 1.0 | 2.0 |
| Intrinsic value | 3 | 1.5 | 4.5 |
| **Total** |  |  | **21.2** |

This explains card values such as `score: 10.3 / 21.2 (49%)`.

```text
normalized Buffett score = weighted earned points / 21.2
```

- Score hint is bullish at 70% or higher.
- Score hint is bearish at 30% or lower.
- Score hint is neutral between them.

### Main component rules

- ROE: full score when ROE exceeds 15% in at least 70% of periods.
- Margins: one point each when average gross, operating, and net margin exceed 20%.
- Debt/equity: full score below 0.30; partial below 0.50.
- Current ratio: full score at 2.0 or above.
- Gross-margin stability: full score when at least 70% of sequential comparisons improve; partial when average gross margin exceeds 30%.
- Capital intensity: full score when average capex/revenue is below 5%; partial below 10%.
- Book value: full score when at least 70% of period comparisons grow; partial at 50%.
- Share count: full score when latest shares are more than 5% below the oldest observation; partial when broadly stable.
- FCF conversion: full score when average FCF/net income exceeds 1.0; partial above 0.8.

### Buffett intrinsic-value sub-score

The Buffett agent uses a separate, simpler valuation from the dedicated Valuation Analyst:

```text
project latest FCF for 5 years at 5% growth
discount each year at 10%
terminal value = year-5 FCF × 15
discount terminal value at 10%
margin of safety = (intrinsic value - market cap) / market cap
```

- Margin > 30%: `3/3`.
- Margin between 0% and 30%: `2/3`.
- Margin between -20% and 0%: `1/3`.
- Margin ≤ -20%: `0/3`.

### Final LLM result

The full scorecard and its bullish/neutral/bearish hint are sent to an LLM with a Buffett-style philosophy prompt. The LLM returns the final direction, confidence, and reasoning.

This means:

> The deterministic threshold creates a hint, but it does not enforce the final direction or confidence.

If the LLM is unavailable through the normal fallback path, the score hint is used at 50% confidence.

### Audit notes

- Missing data usually earns zero while the component's maximum remains in the denominator, making missing data a penalty.
- Gross-margin stability expects a `gross_margin` line item, but the default line-item snapshot requests `gross_profit` instead. This component can therefore remain at zero unless a source supplies the extra field.
- The LLM may disagree with the deterministic hint, and no code guard prevents that override.
- News and insider data are included in the view, but the active scorecard does not score them. The prompt only states the number of news items and insider trades, not their contents.
- The Buffett intrinsic-value model and dedicated Valuation Analyst use different formulas and assumptions. Their values should not be expected to match.
- The label “Warren Buffett” describes an encoded investment style; it is not a representation of Buffett's actual opinion.

# Part II — From analysts to the card

## 15. Consensus

Only signals with status `ok` participate. Failed and abstained signals are recorded but excluded from both numerator and denominator.

For each participating analyst:

- bullish contributes `+confidence`;
- bearish contributes `-confidence`;
- neutral contributes `0`.

```text
consensus weighted score = sum of signed confidence / participating analyst count
```

- Bullish when score > `+0.10`.
- Bearish when score < `-0.10`.
- Neutral otherwise.

All analysts have equal structural weight in the denominator. There is no separate council weight saying, for example, Fundamentals matters twice as much as News. Confidence changes the signed contribution, but every participating analyst adds one to the denominator.

### Consensus confidence

```text
consensus confidence = sum of all participating analyst confidences
                       / participating analyst count
```

Neutral analysts are included. Direction is ignored in this formula.

Therefore, a divided council can have high consensus confidence when its individual analysts are strongly confident in opposing views.

### Worked example

Suppose 11 analysts return:

- four bullish confidences totaling `3.05`;
- one bearish confidence of `0.95`; and
- six neutral analysts, each at `0.50`.

Then:

```text
weighted score = (3.05 - 0.95) / 11 = +0.191
direction = bullish
STARS = 4, Buy

consensus confidence = (3.05 + 0.95 + 6 × 0.50) / 11
                     = 63.6%
```

The `64%` says the average analyst self-reported confidence was 64%. It does not say the bullish consensus has a 64% probability of being correct.

### Audit notes

- Neutral opinions dilute the directional score but can raise consensus confidence.
- Excluding failed and abstained analysts can make the remaining opinions more influential.
- Correlated analysts are not de-duplicated. Fundamentals, Growth, Valuation, and Buffett all reuse overlapping financial data.
- Confidence scales are not calibrated across analysts, yet the aggregator treats them as comparable.

## 16. STARS rating

STARS is a label applied directly to the consensus weighted score.

| Weighted score | STARS | Label |
|---:|---:|---|
| ≥ +0.40 | 5 | Strong Buy |
| ≥ +0.10 and < +0.40 | 4 | Buy |
| > -0.10 and < +0.10 | 3 | Hold |
| > -0.40 and ≤ -0.10 | 2 | Sell |
| ≤ -0.40 | 1 | Strong Sell |

### Audit note

There is a boundary mismatch: consensus direction requires a score strictly greater than `+0.10` or strictly less than `-0.10`, while STARS changes at exactly `±0.10`. At exactly `+0.10`, the direction can be neutral while STARS says Buy; at exactly `-0.10`, direction can be neutral while STARS says Sell. Exact boundaries are uncommon with floating-point inputs, but the rules should still be aligned.

## 17. Risk Manager

### Question

What is the maximum position size allowed by volatility, portfolio correlation, current exposure, and cash?

### Portfolio value

```text
portfolio value = cash + market value of longs - market value of shorts
```

### Volatility

Daily returns use the latest 60 trading days where available.

```text
daily volatility = standard deviation of daily returns
annualized volatility = daily volatility × √252
```

### Base position limit

The base allocation is 20%, multiplied by a volatility factor.

| Annualized volatility | Volatility multiplier | Approximate base limit |
|---|---:|---:|
| < 15% | 1.25 | 25% |
| 15% to < 30% | `1 - 0.5 × (vol - 15%)` | about 20% to 18.5% |
| 30% to < 50% | `0.75 - 0.5 × (vol - 30%)` | about 15% to 13% |
| ≥ 50% | 0.50 | 10% |

The volatility multiplier is clipped between 0.25 and 1.25.

### Correlation adjustment

Average correlation is calculated against active portfolio positions when possible, otherwise against other requested tickers.

| Average correlation | Multiplier |
|---:|---:|
| ≥ 0.80 | 0.70× |
| 0.60 to < 0.80 | 0.85× |
| 0.40 to < 0.60 | 1.00× |
| 0.20 to < 0.40 | 1.05× |
| < 0.20 | 1.10× |

```text
combined allocation = base allocation × correlation multiplier
position limit = portfolio value × combined allocation
remaining limit = position limit - absolute current exposure
available dollars = min(remaining limit, cash)
max shares = floor(available dollars / current price)
```

### Audit notes

- The comments describing some volatility ranges do not match the implemented arithmetic. The table above reflects the code.
- There are discontinuities at 30% and 50% volatility.
- A low-volatility, low-correlation stock can reach `25% × 1.10 = 27.5%`, despite the stated 25% upper intent.
- Missing price data forces current price and available size to zero, preventing a trade.
- Available dollars are capped by cash, so this stage does not use margin for a new long.
- Portfolio positions outside the requested snapshot may lack prices and can be valued at zero.

## 18. Portfolio Manager

### Question

Given consensus and the risk cap, what action and quantity should appear on the card?

### Research and long-only modes

- Bullish with room: buy.
- Bullish without room: hold.
- Bearish with an existing long: sell.
- Bearish without an existing long: hold.
- Neutral: hold.

For a buy:

```text
target dollars = risk manager's available dollars × consensus confidence
quantity = floor(target dollars / current price)
```

The action confidence displayed on the card is copied from consensus confidence.

### Long/short mode

The manager identifies all buy decisions and all bearish tickers, then pairs the most confident buy with the most confident bear.

```text
short quantity = floor(long notional / short price)
hedge ratio = short notional / long notional
```

- Hedge ratio ≥ 0.90 is labeled balanced.
- A smaller positive ratio is partially hedged.
- If there is a buy but no bearish candidate, all opening buys are blocked.

### Audit notes

- Research and long-only behavior are currently the same in this decision function.
- A bearish stock with no existing long is a hold; the normal per-ticker rule never directly opens a short.
- A sell decision carries quantity zero with a “quantity TBD” code comment; the quantity is not filled in within this manager.
- `has_short` is passed into the decision helper but not used.
- Long/short hedge quantity is not capped by the bearish ticker's `max_shares`.
- The bearish ticker's normal per-ticker action remains hold even when it appears as the short leg inside the separate hedge plan.
- When several bullish buys exist, only the strongest buy receives the hedge pair; other buy decisions are not automatically blocked or hedged.
- Position size uses consensus confidence, whose current meaning is average analyst self-confidence rather than directional strength.

# Part III — Debate numbers

## 19. What Debate confidence and conviction mean

Debate does not use the Signals formulas above.

Each specialist produces:

- a stance: bullish, bearish, or neutral;
- 3–5 findings;
- evidence and a source for each finding; and
- finding confidence: high, medium, or low.

A bear model then produces refutations with high/medium/low severity. The synthesizer reviews the specialist drafts and bear case and produces:

- conviction: high, medium, or low;
- directional lean: constructive, neutral, or cautious; and
- bull, bear, and base cases.

These labels are LLM judgments. The synthesizer is instructed to weight evidence quality and recency rather than average specialist votes. High conviction is disallowed by prompt when a high-severity bear risk remains unrebutted, but this is a model instruction rather than a deterministic code check.

If synthesis fails, the degraded fallback counts bullish and bearish specialist stances, sets conviction to low, and derives lean from the majority.

### Citation audit

For filing-based findings:

```text
grounded rate = findings whose cited chunk ID exists / total findings
supported of grounded = supported findings / grounded findings
citation accuracy = supported findings / total findings
```

Support is judged by a small LLM when available. The fallback uses the fraction of evidence tokens found in the cited passage and requires at least 30% overlap.

### Audit notes

- High/medium/low are ordinal labels, not probabilities.
- Web-source debates do not receive the filing chunk-ID citation audit.
- Citation grounding proves that a source exists; support attempts to test attribution. Neither proves the investment conclusion is correct.
- Model, prompt, source recency, and research tier can all change the result.

# Part IV — Interpretation guide

## 20. How to read a signal card correctly

Read the card in this order:

1. **Status coverage:** Which analysts completed, abstained, or failed?
2. **Votes:** How many bullish, bearish, and neutral views exist?
3. **Weighted score:** How directional is the complete council after confidence and neutral dilution?
4. **STARS:** A label for that same weighted score.
5. **Analyst evidence:** Which formulas or sources produced each vote?
6. **Opposing views:** Is one high-confidence analyst disagreeing for a defensible reason?
7. **Risk limit:** Even if the thesis is strong, what size is permitted?
8. **Action:** Did portfolio state or mode turn a research view into buy, sell, or hold?

Do not read the card as:

> “Nine independent models agree there is a 64% chance of a 12-month gain.”

A more accurate reading is:

> “Nine selected lenses produced these rule-based or model-assisted opinions from one saved evidence snapshot. Their signed-confidence average crossed the bullish threshold, their average self-reported confidence was 64%, and the risk/portfolio rules translated that into this action.”

## 21. Reliability hierarchy

This is a practical interpretation hierarchy, not a performance claim.

### Most directly auditable

- Raw prices, financial metrics, line items, and current market cap.
- Individual deterministic sub-scores and threshold comparisons.
- SEC and web citation links.
- Saved snapshot and exact run artifacts.

### Model-dependent but inspectable

- DCF and intrinsic values, because assumptions dominate the output.
- Consensus and STARS, because confidence scales and analyst overlap matter.
- Risk limits, because the formulas are deterministic but heuristic.

### Most judgment-dependent

- Buffett final direction/confidence.
- Adaptive Research direction/confidence.
- Debate specialist confidence and memo conviction.
- Optional LLM explanations.

These are not cosmetic changes; they affect interpretation or output quality.

5. Calibrate analyst confidence scales against historical outcomes before treating percentages as comparable.

## 23. Formula glossary

- **ROE:** net income divided by shareholder equity.
- **Debt/equity:** debt divided by shareholder equity.
- **Current ratio:** current assets divided by current liabilities.
- **FCF:** cash generated after capital expenditure.
- **DCF:** present value of forecast future cash flows.
- **WACC:** blended required return for equity and debt capital.
- **Cost of equity:** return shareholders require for bearing equity risk.
- **Terminal value:** estimated value of cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period.
- **Market cap:** share price multiplied by shares outstanding; market value of equity.
- **Enterprise value:** equity value plus net debt and related claims.
- **Margin of safety:** model value relative to market price, often with an additional haircut.
- **ADX:** trend-strength indicator; it does not itself identify direction.
- **Z-score:** distance from a historical mean measured in standard deviations.
- **Hurst exponent:** heuristic measure of trending versus mean-reverting behavior.
- **Correlation:** degree to which two return series move together.

## Final takeaway

AlphaDesk is best understood as an auditable council of heterogeneous lenses, not as one probability model. Its strongest property is that the evidence, formulas, prompts, and outputs are saved and inspectable. Its largest current methodology risk is that differently constructed confidence scores are displayed on one common percentage scale and then combined as if they were directly comparable.

The right product framing is therefore:

> “Here is how several explicit research methods interpreted the same evidence, how strongly each method crossed its own threshold, where they disagree, and what deterministic risk rules permit.”

That framing is accurate, useful, and honest about what the numbers can and cannot claim.

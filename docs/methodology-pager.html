How Nova Researches a Stock — and How Far It Goes

Nova automates the analytical core of professional equity research, modeled directly on the Standard & Poor's bottom-up methodology. This pager explains what the system does, how it works, and — honestly — how much of a human analyst's job it covers today.

The short version

A human equity analyst follows a roughly 11-step process: read a company's filings, talk to its management, build an earnings forecast, size up the competition, value the business, set a price target and rating, and then keep re-checking it over time. Nova reproduces the analytical spine of that process — the reading, the valuation, the recommendation, and a built-in challenge to the thesis — while the parts that depend on human relationships (calling executives) sit outside what any software can do.

~43%of a full analyst's job, automated end-to-end (counting steps no software can do)
~51%of the automatable job — the fairer measure of the system itself

Two engines do the work

Nova answers any question about a stock with two complementary systems. They cover different halves of the analyst's job — one is the quant, one is the research team.

⚙️ Signals — the quant analyst

Ten deterministic agents crunch the numbers: a four-method intrinsic valuation (DCF, owner earnings, EV/EBITDA, residual income), fundamentals, growth, technicals, news and insider sentiment, and a "Warren Buffett" scoring persona. Deterministic means the same inputs always produce the same answer — it is auditable and reproducible, which matters for a regulated discipline.

💬 Debate — the research pod

An AI team that reasons like analysts arguing in a room: a supervisor breaks the question into mandates, four specialists (fundamental, sentiment, valuation, macro) research SEC filings and the live web, a "bear" challenger attacks the thesis, and a synthesizer judges the evidence and writes a cited verdict. Every claim carries a source.

What it has genuinely built BUILT

  • Reads the filings (Step 1). Fetches real SEC 10-K/10-Q documents, finds the relevant passages, and cites them back to the source.
  • Values the company (Step 7). A proper multi-method discounted-cash-flow model with bear / base / bull scenarios — real finance, not hand-waving.
  • Sets a price target (Step 8). Now produces an explicit 12-month target price — fair value carried forward one year at the cost of equity — with the upside versus today's price.
  • Makes the call (Step 9). Issues a 1–5 STARS rating (Strong Buy … Strong Sell) plus a confidence-scored Buy / Sell / Hold — direct parity with S&P's STARS.
  • Stress-tests the thesis (Step 10). The bear-versus-synthesizer debate is an adversarial review that goes deeper than S&P's static chart even depicts.

Where it is partial or missing PARTIAL GAP

  • Talking to management (Steps 2 & 4) — human-only. Calling executives and validating forecasts with them is a relationship, not a computation. Out of scope by nature.
  • Earnings forecast & competitive analysis (Steps 3, 5, 6). Nova projects cash flows and assesses competition, but does not yet publish a clean 3-year EPS model, a structured five-forces analysis, or the S&P "Core Earnings" accounting clean-up.
  • Ongoing re-coverage (Step 11). Every run is saved, but there is no scheduler that automatically re-checks a stock each quarter.

The path to bigger numbers

Done already: the two quickest wins are now shipped. The 12-month target price (Step 8) and the 1–5 STARS rating (Step 9) were nearly free — the valuation math already existed, it simply wasn't surfaced — and they lift overall coverage from ~38% to ~43% (full job) and ~45% to ~51% (automatable), flipping both boxes to solidly "Built."

Next, to push further:

  • Peer relative valuation (Step 7) and Core Earnings normalization (Step 6) — bounded, days-not-weeks additions that complete the valuation story (toward ~60%+).
  • Structured five-forces (Step 5) and a forward 3-year EPS model (Step 3) — mostly prompt/derivation work on data we already pull.
  • Scheduled re-coverage (Step 11) — the heaviest item (real infrastructure), best saved for last.
